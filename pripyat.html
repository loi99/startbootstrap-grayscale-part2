The Lost City of Pripyat

How did this beautiful thriving city become wasteland?

During the nuclear disaster in Chernobyl, 1986, the radioactive waste travelled far and wide, unfortunately, evacuating over 200,000 residents from their homes in Pripyat.

The Disaster of Chernobyl

In 1986 a sudden surge of power caused a nuclear reactor to explode, the fire that followed released huge amounts of radioactive material into the surrounding environment. A few weeks later the crews completely covered the unit with a temporary concrete structure they called "sarcophagus" in the hopes of limiting any further releases of radioactive material. Moreover, the Soviet government went on to cut down a square mile of trees near the power plant to reduce radioactive contamination. The Soviet and Russian government closed off the area within 18 miles and evacuated 115,000 people and another 220,000 in later years. This formed the start of Pripyats final days of inhabition..

The lost City of Pripyat

Pripyat was officially named a city in in 1979 and inhabited a population of 49,360 by the time of evacuation in 1986. The city was built to house and accomidate workers of the Chernobyl power plant, the city included a hospital, school, an amusement park, a swimming pool and much more. As a reslut of the nuclear disaster, Pripyat was amongst the first cities to be evacuated just a day after the news broke. Today, areas of Pripyats amusement park are still considered to be radioactive, this is because the helicopters removing radioactive soil used this park to land meaning residual soil was washed into the ground. However, you are able to tour the lost city providing you follow a guides instruction to not touch anything.
